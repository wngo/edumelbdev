Creativity the arts and young people

Anthony Colangelo, Bachelor of Arts

Youth / Arts: Expressing Cultural Identity

“Youth Arts was a perfect subject for me to take because it allows students to explore and use their own creative passions in order to better understand the meaning and significance of the place of youth in the world.

Given most university students and subjects are geared toward producing work with an eye on one’s future, it was refreshing to be able to stop, think and analyse the way certain areas of the arts operate within and around our lives. This was taught within a context that we are familiar with and currently experiencing.

Also beneficial and enjoyable was the hands-on nature of the subject, whether that was taking part in drama games, playing music or drawing!”

Creative projects: digital technologies

Availability: Semester 2

Contact hours: 1 hour lecture and 2 hour workshop per week

Level: 1

Assessment details:
  • tutorial presentation (750 words), 20%, due week 5;
  • software review (750 words), 20% due week 9;
  • project with reflective journal (1500 words), 60%, due end of semester

Explore the creative use of digital technologies using computers, mobile devices and software applications like iPhoto, Garageband, iMovie, Final Cut, Movie Maker and the Adobe Creative suite. You will have opportunity to produce items like videos, animations, photographic portfolios, web pages, blogs and music. Discover, explore and review digital artifacts like Vimeo, Creative Commons and Sound Cloud.

Even with little formal arts background you can explore creativity, design and innovation in video and film, drama, music, digital storytelling, web design, blogging and the visual arts, using digital technologies in workshops, seminars and during fieldwork.

You will develop skills in project planning and management, and investigate themes such as online arts communities and artistic collaboration, remix cultures and the digital revolution.

Further information: Andrew Swainston: swaa@unimelb.edu.au or see the Handbook.

Creativity, play and the arts

Availability: Semester 1, Semester 2

Contact hours: I hour lecture and 2 hour workshop per week

Level: 1

Assessment details:
  • observation description (1,200 words), 30%, week 8;
  • observation analysis (1,200 words), 30%, week 8;
  • design (600 word equivalent plus 10 minute presentation), 40%, week 12

Discover the close connections between play, the arts, creativity and learning. Through the arts, children play and imagine, satisfying a fundamental human need to explore, express and create. This practice-based subject, informed by direct observations undertaken in an early learning centre and other sites, explores how children and young people play and artfully create in diverse contexts.

You will explore how children and young people interact with each other and their environment through play, visiting a range of sites in the community and participating in practical arts workshops, involving drama, visual arts and music. You will also develop your own designs and strategies for promoting artful and creative play.

Further information: Robert Brown: r.brown@unimelb.edu.au or see the Handbook.

Story and the arts

Availability: Semester 2

Contact hours: One hour lecture and two hour workshop per week

Level: 2

Assessment details:
  • presentation (1,600 words or equivalent), 40%, presentations take place throughout semester;
  • negotiated project (2,400 words or equivalent) 60%, due end of semester

You will explore the idea of story from its earliest, oral origins to its most contemporary expressions, revisit the oldest stories of folk and fairy tale and their continuing resonances, analyse written, visual and dramatic expressions of narrative, and consider why humans are the only species to create stories.

You will also explore the role of story and narrative in your own life and share this with class members, discuss the role of the audience, and create written, visual, oral and dramatic texts.

A practical and theoretical study of how humans construct and communicate meaning through story, across the artistic forms of drama, literature, visual arts, music and film. Assessment includes a practical creative project.

Further information: Pam Macintyre: p.macintyre@unimelb.edu.au or see the Handbook.

Youth / arts: expressing cultural identities

Availability: Semester 1

Contact hours: 3 hour workshop every week

Level: 2

Assessment details:
  • street research project (1,600 words plus video, still photograph, journal entries and MP3 sound recordings), 40%, due week 8;
  • final paper and exhibition contribution (2,400 words plus student -produced 'artefacts', such as installations, street drama, video production, music performance, internet site construction), 60%, due end of semester

Explore how young people create their social and personal identities through artistic media. Visit art spaces and other cultural sites, conduct a ‘street research’ project and contribute to an exhibition of ‘artefacts’ such as visual art works, musical compositions and dramatic performances.

This subject gives you the chance to work with leading arts practitioners and educators and explore the relationships between the arts and young people. You will take part in weekly arts workshops, covering areas like visual arts, music and the performing arts and attend arts events for young people.

Further information: Dr Richard Sallis: sallis@unimelb.edu.au or see the Handbook.
